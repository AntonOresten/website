---
title: Coherent Record of Thought
description: A chance for manifold musings to manifest
date: 2026-02-07
---

An idea is literally not part of our world until we learn of it. It can affect us externally, but until the moment of contact, it isn't part of our world model. We constantly enter new worlds as new ideas find us. From then on, they live in our heads, occasionally surface in a conversation, but sometimes they dissolve. They pass through us without ever passing through anything else, and there's no record left behind to show they ever did.

I want to take a moment to think about what remains. My thoughts, as they stand, are distributed across relationships, direct messages, group chats, and conversations with language models that may or may not be fed into the next generation of such models—a strange form of accidental immortality. But it's scattered, contextual, and co-shaped by whoever I was talking to at the time, as well as the time itself.

The version of me that exists in any given relationship is real, but partial. We show distinct parts of ourselves to different people, and each person gets a genuine slice but never the whole thing. A platform for pure expression is peculiar in that those limitations fall away. There's ideally no audience to perform for, no dynamic to navigate. Just the thought itself.

---

I want this to be a place to think out loud, but not a place to settle. There's a version of this that goes wrong, where writing things down starts to feel like committing to them. Where you attach yourself too tightly to something and then lose interest, or it changes, and you can't relate to yourself anymore.

Kegan's model helps me name the risk. It's easy to stay in a socialized mode, where your thinking is quietly organized by the audience in front of you. If the room rewards certainty, you become certain. If the room rewards being the "X person", you become that. Writing can either reinforce that, or help move toward a self-authoring stance where beliefs are tools, not identity.

Storr makes a related point in The Status Game: status is always in the room, even when we pretend it's not. The danger for writing like this is specific. A take can start as curiosity and end as a position you defend because it became yours, because people came to associate *you* with *it*, because abandoning it would feel like losing ground. That's the status game playing out at the level of ideas. I've watched that happen, and I can do it too. So I'd rather keep things fuzzy around the edges. Not vague, just unpossessed.

---

*Coherent*. Not coherent as in chronically consistent—I hope to change my mind often. Changing your mind is a good sign because it means you're still in contact with the world. The world is messy, and as the basis changes, so must our ideas and models. I mean coherent as in *a record that holds together enough to show a trajectory*. Fuzzy at the edges, but with enough shape that you can see where the thinking moved.

It's worth distinguishing between identifying with a stance and the stance itself. I can write something down today and outgrow it by next year, even though I hope to be grounded enough to not require 180-degree turns.

---

For now, commitment can be small but consistent. Not necessarily a post every week. Just time set aside for the practice of thinking deliberately, with occasional sprints of creativity.

I'm talking to the void. Sometimes the void talks back. If it doesn't, the thoughts still exist somewhere outside my head now, and that's enough.
