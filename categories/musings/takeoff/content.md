---
title: A Functionally Hard Takeoff
date: 2026-03-06
---

What if you don't need an intelligence explosion to get a functionally hard takeoff? In the original sense, hard takeoff refers to a steep, self-reinforcing acceleration in capability. A functionally hard takeoff is different. It occurs when the interval between major capability gains is shorter than the time institutions need to understand, evaluate, and govern them — and when each advance changes what governance would need to look like. The problem is not speed alone. It is that the target moves before anyone can aim.

Under current political conditions, governance is not impossible, but it is structurally trailing. The EU AI Act was proposed in 2021, entered into force in 2024, and most of its rules apply only later, with key provisions stretching into mid-2026 and 2027 ([EU AI Act timeline](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai)). This is not unusual — it is how regulation works. Evaluation standards require empirical evidence of harm, which requires deployment, which means oversight is inherently posterior to the thing it oversees. Even where political will exists, the structural lag remains. The rapid oscillation of US AI policy between administrations illustrates the point: urgency alone does not produce durable governance if the underlying capability keeps shifting.

Institutions are not failing. They are operating on timescales that made sense before the interval between capability jumps shortened to the point where each new generation redefines the problem the previous framework was designed to solve. Regulators end up governing the last system, every time — not because they are slow, but because the object of regulation is unstable. This is the core mechanism: governance is not merely late, it is chasing a moving target. Competitive pressure compounds the problem. Deployment is faster than deliberation by design, and in the absence of unusual coordination, more powerful systems will continue entering the world before anyone has defined what "under control" would require.

METR's task-length data — which tracks the duration of tasks frontier AI systems can complete autonomously — suggests a doubling time of around seven months ([METR](https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/)). The metric becomes less meaningful at longer horizons, where task length is a poor proxy for capability. But the argument does not depend on a specific doubling time or a specific metric. Any consistent pace of improvement, even a decelerating one, can outrun governance if institutional response times are long enough. What matters is whether the interval between advances remains shorter than the time required to govern them. For now, it does.

Compute governance is the clearest test case. It is one of the few upstream levers capable of stretching the interval between frontier capability jumps, and it illustrates exactly why even the most promising tools are subject to the same institutional lag. The obvious version — throttle only frontier-scale training runs at the largest labs — is more targeted than a blanket restriction, but harder to implement than it appears. Threshold-based rules, such as the 10²⁶ FLOP trigger in the 2023 US executive order ([R47843](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47843)), require knowing what is being trained, which is a surveillance and verification problem that no one has solved at scale, let alone internationally. Targeted domestic restrictions risk shifting the frontier toward actors with less safety infrastructure: smaller labs, state programs, or open-weight releases beyond regulatory reach. And the lever itself is loosening. As algorithmic efficiency improves, compute becomes a less reliable proxy for capability — the ceiling on what can be done with a fixed compute budget keeps rising. The strongest version of the concern is not that compute governance creates regulatory capture, but that national-level compute governance is leaky unless it is coordinated internationally, and international coordination is itself subject to the same institutional lag the essay describes. The problem is recursive.

Governance frameworks will keep arriving to regulate systems that have already been superseded. The discontinuity is not in the capabilities themselves but in our ability to track, evaluate, and govern them. At some point, even the developers of frontier systems lose the ability to predict what the next generation will be capable of — scaling-law surprises and emergent behaviors suggest this is not hypothetical. When the builders cannot anticipate what they are building, and the regulators cannot keep pace with what has already been built, the result is an awareness gap that is not metaphorical but measurable. In that sense, it is a singularity less with respect to time than to awareness.
