---
title: A Functionally Hard Takeoff
date: 2026-03-06
---

What if you don't need an intelligence explosion to get a functionally hard takeoff? Under current political conditions, the problem is not that governance is impossible, but that deployment, incentives, and institutional learning move more slowly than capability gains. The EU AI Act was proposed in 2021, entered into force in 2024, and most of its rules apply later, with key provisions stretching into mid 2026 and 2027 ([EU AI Act timeline](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai)). That gap is contingent, not inevitable; governments can slow or redirect deployment. But unless they act with unusual speed, clarity, and coordination, capabilities will keep advancing faster than institutions can absorb them. Competitive pressure pushes more powerful systems into the world before anyone can define what “under control” would require. 

In the original sense, hard takeoff means a steep acceleration in capability itself. This argument is different. METR's task-length data — which tracks the duration of tasks frontier AI systems can complete autonomously — shows a doubling time of around seven months ([METR](https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/)). The metric becomes less meaningful at longer horizons, where task length is a poor proxy for capability. But as a signal of pace, the trend is clear enough: capability is advancing faster than institutions can absorb it. Even if that exact rate shifts, there can still be a functionally hard takeoff in governance terms: a state in which the interval between important capability jumps is shorter than the time institutions need to understand, evaluate, and govern them.

Under that framing, we are already knee deep. The real overhang is not just technical but institutional. Compute is — or at least has been — one of the few upstream levers that can slow frontier capability jumps and buy time for oversight. If institutions never close the gap between successive advances, it will resemble hard takeoff even without a singularity-style rupture. The danger is not only that control erodes, but that each step is absorbed as normal before its implications have been understood. It's a singularity not with respect to time, but legislation.
