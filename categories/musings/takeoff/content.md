---
title: A Functionally Hard Takeoff
date: 2026-03-06
---

What if you do not need an intelligence explosion to get something functionally like hard takeoff? Under current political conditions, the problem is not that governance is impossible, but that deployment, incentives, and institutional learning move more slowly than capability gains. The EU AI Act was proposed in 2021, entered into force in 2024, and most of its rules apply later, with key provisions stretching into 2026 and 2027 ([EU AI Act timeline](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai)). That gap is contingent, not inevitable; governments can slow or redirect deployment. But unless they act with unusual speed, clarity, and coordination, capabilities will keep advancing faster than institutions can absorb them. Competitive pressure pushes more powerful systems into the world before anyone can define what “under control” would require. 

In the original sense, hard takeoff means a steep acceleration in capability itself. This argument is different. According to METR, the length of tasks frontier AI systems can complete has been rising with a doubling time of around seven months ([METR](https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/)). Even if that exact rate shifts, there can still be a functionally hard takeoff in governance terms: a condition in which the interval between important capability jumps is shorter than the time institutions need to understand, evaluate, and govern them. The relevant curve is not capability alone, but capability relative to society’s ability to metabolize it. What makes the situation hard is not that control is impossible, but that institutions may fail to reestablish it between one jump and the next.s

Under that framing, we may already be knee deep. The real overhang is not just technical but institutional. Compute matters not as a kill switch for intelligence, but as one of the few upstream levers that can slow frontier capability jumps and buy time for oversight. The deeper point is simpler: if institutions never close the gap between successive advances, AI will functionally resemble hard takeoff even without a singularity-style rupture.
