---
title: A Functionally Hard Takeoff
date: 2026-03-06
---

What if you don't need an intelligence explosion to get a functionally hard takeoff? In the original sense, hard takeoff refers to a steep acceleration in capability itself. By contrast, a functionally hard takeoff occurs when the interval between major capability gains is shorter than the time institutions need to understand, evaluate, and govern them.

Under current political conditions, the problem is not that governance is impossible. It is that deployment, incentives, and institutional learning move more slowly than capability gains. The EU AI Act was proposed in 2021, entered into force in 2024, and most of its rules apply only later, with key provisions stretching into mid-2026 and 2027 ([EU AI Act timeline](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai)). The gap is contingent, not inevitable; governments can slow or redirect deployment. But unless they act with unusual speed, clarity, and coordination, competitive pressure will continue pushing more powerful systems into the world before anyone has defined what "under control" would require.

Institutions won't keep pace. That isn't a prediction about their competence; it's a description of the incentive structure. Deployment is faster than deliberation by design, and each new generation of capability redefines the problem oversight was trying to solve. By the time a framework matures enough to apply, the capabilities it targets have been superseded by ones that require fundamentally different assumptions. Regulators end up governing the previous system, every time.

METR's task-length data — which tracks the duration of tasks frontier AI systems can complete autonomously — suggests a doubling time of around seven months ([METR](https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/)). The metric becomes less meaningful at longer horizons, where task length is a poor proxy for capability, but it is a good signal of pace. This does resemble an actual hard takeoff, but the argument doesn't depend on a specific doubling time. What matters is whether the interval between advances remains shorter than the time required to govern them. For now, it is.

Compute is, or at least has been, one of the few upstream levers capable of slowing frontier capability jumps and stretching the interval between advances. The major frontier labs and cloud providers already sit on enormous compute stockpiles. Throttling access now would consolidate their position — a form of regulatory capture where the barrier to entry is enforced by the very policy meant to ensure safety. The entities best positioned to benefit from compute governance are the ones least likely to be constrained by it.

Governance frameworks will keep arriving to regulate systems that have already been replaced by more capable successors. The discontinuity isn't in the capabilities themselves but in our ability to track, evaluate, and govern them. In that sense, it's a singularity less with respect to time than to awareness.
