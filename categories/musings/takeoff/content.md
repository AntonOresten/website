---
title: A Functionally Hard Takeoff
date: 2026-03-06
---

What if you do not need an intelligence explosion to get a functionally hard takeoff? What if compounding adoption failure is enough? We are already behind on understanding and governing what AI can do, and deployment, incentives, and institutional learning move at different speeds. That gap is not inevitable; governments can slow, constrain, and sometimes sharply redirect deployment. But unless they act with unusual speed, clarity, and coordination, capability gains will keep arriving faster than institutions can absorb them. Competitive pressure keeps pushing more powerful systems into the world before anyone can say, in a meaningful sense, what “under control” would require.

Under those conditions, even smooth capability growth can produce something that feels a lot like hard takeoff. Not because intelligence suddenly spikes, but because governance keeps falling behind. The important curve is not capability alone, but capability relative to society's ability to metabolize it. That is also why compute matters: as the clearest upstream lever, it functions less as a kill switch for intelligence than as a throttle on the pace of frontier capability jumps. Even if technical progress turns out to be steadier than the most dramatic scenarios suggest, there can still be a hard takeoff in governance terms: a regime in which institutions never close the gap between one capability jump and the next.

Under that framing, we may already be knee deep. Hard no longer has to name a future threshold where everything suddenly changes. It can also name a condition in which control erodes continuously because each threshold is crossed before the last has been politically, legally, or conceptually digested. The overhang is not waiting somewhere ahead of us. It is here now, in the accumulating distance between what AI can do and what the institutions around it can reliably understand, govern, and contain.
